# The Savior for All: Heaven Rejoices Over One Sinner

Luke 15 is the most famous chapter in Luke — and arguably the heart of the entire Gospel.

The Pharisees and scribes are grumbling: *This man receives sinners and eats with them.*

Jesus answers them with three parables — each one about something lost and then found, each one ending with the same joyful response: *Rejoice with me, for I have found what was lost.*

A lost sheep, out of one hundred.
A lost coin, out of ten.
A lost son, out of two.

The Savior for All is the Shepherd, the Woman with the lamp, the Father waiting on the road. And heaven rejoices over every sinner who comes home.

## The grumbling Pharisees

Now the tax collectors and sinners are all drawing near to hear Him. And the Pharisees and the scribes grumble:

> "This man receives sinners and eats with them."

It is the same accusation they have made before. They mean it as condemnation. Jesus turns it into the title of the gospel.

He receives sinners. He eats with them. That is the gospel.

## The lost sheep

He tells them this parable:

> "What man of you, having a hundred sheep, if he has lost one of them, does not leave the ninety-nine in the open country, and go after the one that is lost, until he finds it?"

> "And when he has found it, he lays it on his shoulders, rejoicing. And when he comes home, he calls together his friends and his neighbors, saying to them, 'Rejoice with me, for I have found my sheep that was lost.'"

He concludes:

> "Just so, I tell you, there will be more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents than over ninety-nine righteous persons who need no repentance."

**The Savior for All is the Shepherd who goes after the one.** He does not write off the lost sheep as acceptable losses. He goes after it, finds it, carries it home on His shoulders, and throws a party.

## The lost coin

> "What woman, having ten silver coins, if she loses one coin, does not light a lamp and sweep the house and seek diligently until she finds it?"

> "And when she has found it, she calls together her friends and neighbors, saying, 'Rejoice with me, for I have found the coin that I had lost.'"

> "Just so, I tell you, there is joy before the angels of God over one sinner who repents."

The shepherd searched in the open country. The woman searches in the house. She lights a lamp. She sweeps. She searches diligently. The lost coin will not find itself — but the One who lost it will not stop until it is recovered.

## The lost son

The third parable is the longest. It is the most famous story Jesus ever told.

A man had two sons. The younger said to his father:

> "Father, give me the share of property that is coming to me."

This was an unthinkable request. To ask for the inheritance was effectively to say: *I wish you were dead.*

The father divided his property between them.

Not many days later, the younger son gathered all he had and took a journey into a far country. There he squandered his property in reckless living.

When he had spent everything, a severe famine arose in that country, and he began to be in need. He hired himself out to one of the citizens of that country, who sent him into his fields to feed pigs — a Jewish boy feeding pigs, the picture of utter degradation.

He was longing to be fed with the pods that the pigs ate, and no one gave him anything.

## "When he came to himself"

> "But when he came to himself, he said, 'How many of my father's hired servants have more than enough bread, but I perish here with hunger! I will arise and go to my father, and I will say to him, "Father, I have sinned against heaven and before you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son. Treat me as one of your hired servants."'"

He rose and came to his father.

This is repentance. *I have sinned. I am not worthy. Take me back on any terms.*

## The father runs

> "But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and felt compassion, and ran and embraced him and kissed him."

A dignified Middle Eastern father did not run. It was undignified. He would have walked.

This father runs.

He sees his son a long way off. He has been watching. He has been waiting. The moment the son appears on the horizon, the father is off the porch and down the road.

The son begins his rehearsed confession: *Father, I have sinned against heaven and before you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son.*

He never gets to finish.

The father interrupts the servants:

> "Bring quickly the best robe, and put it on him, and put a ring on his hand, and shoes on his feet. And bring the fattened calf and kill it, and let us eat and celebrate. For this my son was dead, and is alive again; he was lost, and is found."

They begin to celebrate.

**The Savior for All is the Father who runs to the returning sinner — and throws a party.** No probation. No half-restoration. No years of working off the debt. Just the robe, the ring, the shoes, the calf, the feast.

## The elder brother

But the older son was in the field. As he came and drew near to the house, he heard music and dancing. He called one of the servants and asked what these things meant.

The servant said: *Your brother has come, and your father has killed the fattened calf, because he has received him back safe and sound.*

The older brother was angry and refused to go in.

His father came out and entreated him.

He answered:

> "Look, these many years I have served you, and I never disobeyed your command, yet you never gave me a young goat, that I might celebrate with my friends. But when this son of yours came, who has devoured your property with prostitutes, you killed the fattened calf for him!"

This is the heart of the Pharisee. *I served you. I never disobeyed. And you celebrate this son of yours.*

Notice: he does not say *my brother.* He says *this son of yours.*

The father answers:

> "Son, you are always with me, and all that is mine is yours. It was fitting to celebrate and be glad, for this your brother was dead, and is alive; he was lost, and is found."

The story ends there. Open-ended. We do not know if the elder brother went in.

**The parable is told for the Pharisees and scribes who grumbled at the beginning of the chapter.** Will they come into the feast and rejoice that sinners are being received? Or will they stand outside in resentment, refusing to share the Father's joy?

The Savior for All has welcomed the prodigal. The question is whether the elder brother will join the party.

---

## Closing prayer

Father, thank You that You are the Shepherd who goes after the one lost sheep, the Woman who lights the lamp and sweeps until she finds the coin, the Father who runs down the road to meet the returning son. Save us from the elder brother's resentment. Let us rejoice when sinners come home — because we ourselves are sinners who have come home. And when we run from You, run after us, and run to us, and put the robe and the ring and the shoes on us, and bring us into the feast. In Jesus' name, amen.
