# The Savior for All: The Widow's Two Coins and the End of the Age

Luke 21 holds two contrasting pictures.

It opens with a poor widow placing two small coins into the temple treasury — and Jesus declaring that she has given more than all the rich combined. It moves into the great prophetic discourse on the destruction of the temple, the signs of the end, and the coming of the Son of Man in clouds with power and great glory.

A widow with two coins and the end of the world. The smallest gift and the largest event. **In both, what God values is what the world overlooks: a heart fully given, and a people awake at His coming.**

## The widow with two coins

Jesus looks up and sees the rich putting their gifts into the offering box. He also sees a poor widow putting in two small copper coins.

He says:

> "Truly, I tell you, this poor widow has put in more than all of them. For they all contributed out of their abundance, but she out of her poverty put in all she had to live on."

**The Savior for All does not measure giving by amount. He measures it by cost.** The rich gave from their surplus. The widow gave from her survival. What looked tiny in the offering box was, in heaven's accounting, the largest gift of the day.

This is the heart of the Kingdom. The widow's two coins are honored. The rich are not even named.

## The temple will fall

While some are speaking of the temple, how it is adorned with noble stones and offerings, He says:

> "As for these things that you see, the days will come when there will not be left here one stone upon another that will not be thrown down."

The temple was the pride of the nation. It was massive. Some stones weighed hundreds of tons. It seemed indestructible.

He says: it will all fall.

In AD 70 — within a generation — Rome would do exactly that.

**What looks permanent to us is temporary to God. Only His Kingdom endures.**

## Signs of the end

They ask Him: "Teacher, when will these things be, and what will be the sign when these things are about to take place?"

He answers carefully. His prophecy interweaves the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70 with the broader pattern of the end of the age. Both serve as previews of one another.

> "See that you are not led astray. For many will come in my name, saying, 'I am he!' and, 'The time is at hand!' Do not go after them."

When you hear of wars and tumults, do not be terrified. These things must first take place, but the end will not be at once.

> "Nation will rise against nation, and kingdom against kingdom. There will be great earthquakes, and in various places famines and pestilences. And there will be terrors and great signs from heaven."

But before all this, they will lay their hands on you and persecute you, delivering you up to the synagogues and prisons. You will be brought before kings and governors for His name's sake.

This will be your opportunity to bear witness.

> "Settle it therefore in your minds not to meditate beforehand how to answer, for I will give you a mouth and wisdom, which none of your adversaries will be able to withstand or contradict."

**The Savior for All does not promise His witnesses safety in this age. He promises His own mouth and wisdom through them — and ultimate vindication.**

> "You will be delivered up even by parents and brothers and relatives and friends, and some of you they will put to death. You will be hated by all for my name's sake. But not a hair of your head will perish. By your endurance you will gain your lives."

## The siege of Jerusalem

> "But when you see Jerusalem surrounded by armies, then know that its desolation has come near. Then let those who are in Judea flee to the mountains, and let those who are inside the city depart, and let not those who are out in the country enter it."

These are days of vengeance, fulfillment of all that is written. Distress upon the earth, wrath against this people. They will fall by the edge of the sword and be led captive among all nations. Jerusalem will be trampled by the Gentiles, until the times of the Gentiles are fulfilled.

This was fulfilled with terrible historical precision in AD 70 under the Roman general Titus.

## The coming of the Son of Man

Then the prophecy widens to the end of all things:

> "There will be signs in sun and moon and stars, and on the earth distress of nations in perplexity because of the roaring of the sea and the waves, people fainting with fear and with foreboding of what is coming on the world. For the powers of the heavens will be shaken."

> "And then they will see the Son of Man coming in a cloud with power and great glory."

To His disciples He says:

> "Now when these things begin to take place, straighten up and raise your heads, because your redemption is drawing near."

When the world is fainting in fear, the disciples are straightening up — because for the believer, the return of the Son of Man is not terror but redemption.

## The lesson of the fig tree

He tells them a parable. Look at the fig tree, and all the trees. As soon as they come out in leaf, you see for yourselves and know that the summer is already near. So also, when you see these things taking place, you know that the Kingdom of God is near.

> "Truly, I say to you, this generation will not pass away until all has taken place."

> "Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away."

This is the great certainty. Everything we can see will end. The words of the Savior will not.

## "Stay awake"

He gives the closing warning:

> "But watch yourselves lest your hearts be weighed down with dissipation and drunkenness and cares of this life, and that day come upon you suddenly like a trap. For it will come upon all who dwell on the face of the whole earth. But stay awake at all times, praying that you may have strength to escape all these things that are going to take place, and to stand before the Son of Man."

**Three dangers will weigh down our hearts: dissipation, drunkenness, and the cares of this life.** The first two are obvious. The third is the silent killer of disciples — the daily anxieties that strangle the soul without our noticing.

The Savior for All warns us: stay awake. Pray. Stand ready.

## In the temple by day, on the mount by night

Every day He is teaching in the temple, but at night He goes out and lodges on the mount called Olivet. Early in the morning all the people come to Him in the temple to hear Him.

The Savior is at His public work by day and His private communion by night. The hours of His ministry are numbered now. The final week has begun. And the widow's two coins stand at the threshold as a quiet picture of what He Himself is about to do — give everything He had to live on.

---

## Closing prayer

Father, save us from giving You only what we can spare. Make us widows with two coins — pouring out everything we have, because we trust the Provider more than the provision. Keep our hearts from being weighed down by dissipation, drunkenness, or the cares of this life. Make us awake and praying, ready to lift our heads and stand before the Son of Man when He comes in clouds with power and great glory. In Jesus' name, amen.
