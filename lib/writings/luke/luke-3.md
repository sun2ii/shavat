# The Savior for All: A Savior for the Whole Human Race

Luke 3 makes the case that the Savior is for everyone — by tracing His family tree all the way back to Adam.

Matthew traced Jesus' genealogy to Abraham, the father of the Jewish people. Luke traces it past Abraham, past every patriarch, past every nation, all the way to Adam. The Son of God is the Son of Man — and not just a Jewish man, but the representative of every man, woman, and child who has ever lived.

The chapter opens with John the Baptist preparing the way. It moves through the baptism of Jesus, where the Father speaks and the Spirit descends. It ends with a genealogy that closes with a stunning phrase: *the son of Adam, the son of God.*

The Savior for All has come for the whole human race.

## The word in the wilderness

> "In the fifteenth year of the reign of Tiberius Caesar… the word of God came to John the son of Zechariah in the wilderness."

Luke is precise. He names the emperor, the governor, the tetrarchs, the high priests. This happened in real history, in a known year, under named rulers.

But notice where the word of God came: not to Tiberius, not to Pilate, not to the high priests — but to John in the wilderness.

**The word of God comes to those who are listening, not to those with the loudest titles.**

## A baptism of repentance

John goes into all the region around the Jordan, proclaiming:

> "A baptism of repentance for the forgiveness of sins."

The way to receive the Savior is to repent. To turn. To stop hiding sin behind religion.

Luke applies Isaiah to John:

> "The voice of one crying in the wilderness: 'Prepare the way of the Lord, make his paths straight. Every valley shall be filled, and every mountain and hill shall be made low… and all flesh shall see the salvation of God.'"

*All flesh.* This is the keyword. The Savior is for everyone, and every barrier will be leveled.

## Bear fruits in keeping with repentance

The crowds come to be baptized. John does not flatter them:

> "You brood of vipers! Who warned you to flee from the wrath to come? Bear fruits in keeping with repentance. And do not begin to say to yourselves, 'We have Abraham as our father.' For I tell you, God is able from these stones to raise up children for Abraham."

Religious heritage does not save. Real repentance bears real fruit.

The people ask: "What then shall we do?"

John gives three practical answers — one for the crowds, one for the tax collectors, one for the soldiers.

To the crowds:

> "Whoever has two tunics is to share with him who has none, and whoever has food is to do likewise."

To the tax collectors:

> "Collect no more than you are authorized to do."

To the soldiers:

> "Do not extort money from anyone by threats or by false accusation, and be content with your wages."

**Repentance is not abstract.** It changes the way you handle your stuff, your job, your power. The Savior for All meets us in the ordinary places.

## One mightier than I

The people are in expectation. They wonder whether John might be the Christ. He answers:

> "I baptize you with water, but he who is mightier than I is coming, the strap of whose sandals I am not worthy to untie. He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and fire."

John knows who he is and who he is not. The herald is not the King. The voice is not the Word.

**True greatness in the Kingdom always points away from itself to the One who is coming.**

## Herod imprisons John

Luke briefly notes that Herod, the tetrarch, was reproved by John for Herodias, his brother's wife, and for all the evil things he had done. Herod added this to all the rest: he locked up John in prison.

The way of the prophet is the way of confrontation. The way of the powerful is the way of silencing the prophet. The Savior who is coming will walk the same road.

## The baptism of Jesus

> "Now when all the people were baptized, and when Jesus also had been baptized and was praying, the heavens were opened, and the Holy Spirit descended on him in bodily form, like a dove; and a voice came from heaven, 'You are my beloved Son; with you I am well pleased.'"

The Trinity is fully present at the Jordan: the Father speaking, the Son being baptized, the Spirit descending.

Notice the order: Jesus is baptized *with the people*. He stands in line with sinners — though He has no sin of His own. He identifies with the very people He has come to save.

And then the Father speaks. Before any miracle. Before any sermon. *You are my beloved Son; with you I am well pleased.*

**The Savior is loved before He serves. The Son is approved before He suffers.** And this same approval becomes ours when we are united to Him.

## The genealogy back to Adam

Then Luke gives the genealogy. He runs the line backward — from Jesus, through Joseph, through Heli, through David, through Abraham, through Noah, through Seth, all the way to:

> "The son of Adam, the son of God."

This is the punchline.

Matthew began with Abraham — pointing to a Jewish Messiah for a Jewish audience. Luke goes all the way back to Adam — pointing to a Savior for the entire human family.

**Every human being is a son or daughter of Adam.** And the Savior has now come as the new Adam — to undo what the first Adam broke, to bring life where there was death, to restore the human race in Himself.

The Savior for All is the Son of Adam — and the Son of God. Both at once. Fully one of us. Fully one with the Father.

---

## Closing prayer

Father, thank You that the Savior is not merely for one nation, one class, one kind of person — He is for the whole human race. Thank You that He stood in line with sinners at the Jordan and identified Himself with us. Make us bear fruits in keeping with repentance, in the way we handle our money and our power and our work. And let us hear the Father's voice over us, in His beloved Son: *with you I am well pleased.* In Jesus' name, amen.
