# The Servant Son of God: Not to Be Served, but to Serve

Mark 10 contains the verse that unlocks the entire Gospel:

> "For even the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many."

Everything Mark has been showing us — the healings, the exorcisms, the feeding, the calming, the teaching — gathers into this one line. The Servant came to serve. And the ultimate service was to give His life.

The whole chapter circles this center: marriage, children, wealth, ambition. In every case, the Servant inverts the values of the world.

## On divorce

The Pharisees test Him: "Is it lawful for a man to divorce his wife?"

Jesus turns them to Moses, then beyond Moses to Genesis:

> "From the beginning of creation, God made them male and female. Therefore a man shall leave his father and mother and hold fast to his wife, and the two shall become one flesh. So they are no longer two but one flesh. What therefore God has joined together, let not man separate."

Moses permitted divorce certificates because of hardness of heart. But the original design was one flesh, permanent and indissoluble.

The Servant Son raises the standard. He does not lower God's design to fit the bent of fallen humanity. He calls us back to creation.

## Let the children come

People are bringing children to Jesus that He might touch them. The disciples rebuke them.

Jesus is indignant:

> "Let the children come to me; do not hinder them, for to such belongs the kingdom of God. Truly, I say to you, whoever does not receive the kingdom of God like a child shall not enter it."

He takes them in His arms and blesses them.

**The Kingdom is not entered by climbing but by being received.** A child cannot earn. A child cannot perform. A child can only trust. That is faith.

The disciples have been wanting to manage who gets to Jesus. The Servant Son will not be managed. He welcomes the ones the world overlooks.

## The rich young ruler

A man runs up and kneels:

> "Good Teacher, what must I do to inherit eternal life?"

Jesus does two things at once. First He probes the word *good* — "Why do you call me good? No one is good except God alone." (Will the man see the implication?)

Then He lists commandments — do not murder, do not commit adultery, do not steal, do not bear false witness, do not defraud, honor your father and mother.

The man answers: "Teacher, all these I have kept from my youth."

Mark adds the heart-wrenching detail: "Jesus, looking at him, loved him."

Then He says:

> "You lack one thing: go, sell all that you have and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven; and come, follow me."

The man's face falls. He goes away sorrowful — for he had great possessions.

**The Servant Son does not flatter the seeker.** He puts a finger on the one thing the man would not surrender — and the man chose his possessions over the Christ who loved him.

## The eye of a needle

Jesus turns to the disciples:

> "How difficult it will be for those who have wealth to enter the kingdom of God! It is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich person to enter the kingdom of God."

The disciples are astonished. In their categories, wealth was a sign of God's blessing. If the rich cannot be saved, who can?

He looks at them:

> "With man it is impossible, but not with God. For all things are possible with God."

Salvation is impossible to man. Salvation is possible to God. This is the gospel.

## The third prediction of the cross

For the third time, Jesus tells them plainly what is coming:

> "See, we are going up to Jerusalem, and the Son of Man will be delivered over to the chief priests and the scribes, and they will condemn him to death and deliver him over to the Gentiles. And they will mock him and spit on him, and flog him and kill him. And after three days he will rise."

Three predictions. Three failures to understand.

## James and John ask for thrones

James and John come and ask:

> "Grant us to sit, one at your right hand and one at your left, in your glory."

The Servant has just predicted the cross. They are angling for thrones.

Jesus answers gently:

> "You do not know what you are asking. Are you able to drink the cup that I drink, or to be baptized with the baptism with which I am baptized?"

They say they are able. He tells them they will indeed share His suffering — but the seats at His right and left are not His to give.

The other ten are indignant — not at the wrong of asking, but because they wanted the seats too.

## The great teaching

Then Jesus gathers them and gives the great teaching:

> "You know that those who are considered rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them, and their great ones exercise authority over them. But it shall not be so among you. Whoever would be great among you must be your servant, and whoever would be first among you must be slave of all."

This is the constitution of His Kingdom. The world ascends by domination. The Kingdom ascends by service.

And then the verse that anchors the whole Gospel:

> "For even the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many."

**The Servant Son did not come for thrones. He came for a cross. He came to give His life as a ransom — a payment to set captives free.** And those who follow Him walk in His footsteps.

## Blind Bartimaeus

As they leave Jericho, blind Bartimaeus sits by the road. He hears that Jesus is passing by and cries out:

> "Jesus, Son of David, have mercy on me!"

The crowd tells him to be silent. He cries all the louder. Jesus stops and calls him.

> "What do you want me to do for you?"

The same question He asked James and John. They wanted thrones. Bartimaeus says:

> "Rabbi, let me recover my sight."

Jesus answers:

> "Go your way; your faith has made you well."

Immediately he recovers his sight and follows Jesus on the way.

**The Servant Son ends the chapter as He has lived it — stopping for the one nobody saw, healing the one nobody helped, calling another follower onto the road to Jerusalem.**

## Closing prayer

Father, save us from grasping for thrones in a Kingdom built by a cross. Make us last of all and servants of all, after the pattern of Your Servant Son. Teach us to receive Your Kingdom like a child, to release whatever wealth grips us, and to cry out for mercy like Bartimaeus. Thank You that the Son of Man came to give His life as a ransom for many — and that we are among the many. In Jesus' name, amen.
