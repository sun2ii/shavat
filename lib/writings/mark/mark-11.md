# The Servant Son of God: The King Enters His City

Mark 11 begins the final week.

Everything in this chapter is loaded. The triumphal entry is not a victory parade — it is a king arriving at his capital. The cursing of the fig tree is not random — it is a parable in action against fruitless religion. The cleansing of the temple is not a tantrum — it is a judicial act of the Son against a house that has become a den of robbers.

The Servant Son has reached Jerusalem. The week of His passion has begun.

## The colt

He sends two disciples ahead to the village. They will find a colt tied — one no one has ever ridden. If anyone asks, they are to say: "The Lord has need of it."

It happens exactly as He said.

The disciples spread their cloaks on the colt. Jesus sits on it. Others spread cloaks and leafy branches on the road.

This is the fulfillment of Zechariah:

> "Behold, your king is coming to you; righteous and having salvation is he, humble and mounted on a donkey."

He is not riding a war horse. He is riding a colt. Conquerors come on stallions. The Servant Son comes on a borrowed colt. **Humility is the key to His kingship.**

## Hosanna

The crowds cry out:

> "Hosanna! Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord! Blessed is the coming kingdom of our father David! Hosanna in the highest!"

*Hosanna* means *save now*. They are quoting Psalm 118. They sense — even without fully understanding — that the King has arrived.

He enters Jerusalem. He goes into the temple. He looks around at everything. And then — Mark gives us a strange line — He returns to Bethany with the twelve.

He does nothing. He looks. He waits.

The next day, the work begins.

## The cursing of the fig tree

On the way back to the city, Jesus is hungry. He sees in the distance a fig tree in leaf and goes to see if He can find anything on it. He finds nothing but leaves — for it is not the season for figs.

He says to the tree:

> "May no one ever eat fruit from you again."

The disciples hear it.

This is no petty curse. This is a prophetic act. The fig tree, in full leaf and no fruit, is a picture of Israel — and specifically the temple. Full of religious display. Empty of fruit. **The Servant Son does not bless what only looks alive.**

## Cleansing the temple

He enters the temple and begins to drive out those buying and selling. He overturns the tables of the money changers and the seats of those who sell pigeons. He will not allow anyone to carry anything through the temple.

He teaches:

> "Is it not written, 'My house shall be called a house of prayer for all the nations'? But you have made it a den of robbers."

The temple was supposed to be a place where the nations could come and pray. They had turned it into a market — and worse, a place where the poor pilgrim was cheated by inflated exchange rates and overpriced sacrifices.

The Servant Son acts as the rightful Lord of the temple. He is not a reformer with a suggestion. He is the Owner reclaiming His house.

**Religion that exploits the poor and crowds out prayer is religion that the Christ overturns.**

The chief priests and scribes hear of it and seek a way to destroy Him. They fear Him — because the whole crowd is astonished at His teaching.

## The withered fig tree

In the morning, as they pass by, the disciples see the fig tree withered to its roots. Peter remembers:

> "Rabbi, look! The fig tree that you cursed has withered."

Jesus answers:

> "Have faith in God. Truly, I say to you, whoever says to this mountain, 'Be taken up and thrown into the sea,' and does not doubt in his heart, but believes that what he says will come to pass, it will be done for him."

This is one of the great teachings on faith and prayer.

> "Whatever you ask in prayer, believe that you have received it, and it will be yours."

Then He adds a vital qualification:

> "And whenever you stand praying, forgive, if you have anything against anyone, so that your Father also who is in heaven may forgive you your trespasses."

**Prayer that moves mountains is not magic. It is faith joined to a forgiving heart.** You cannot live as an unforgiving person and expect mountain-moving prayer.

## The challenge to His authority

He returns to Jerusalem. As He walks in the temple, the chief priests, scribes, and elders come to Him:

> "By what authority are you doing these things, or who gave you this authority to do them?"

This is the natural question. He has overturned tables in their temple. He has been hailed as the coming King. They want to know on whose authority.

Jesus answers their question with a question:

> "Was the baptism of John from heaven or from man?"

They are trapped. If they say *from heaven*, He will ask why they did not believe him. If they say *from man*, the crowd will be furious — for they hold John to have been a prophet.

They answer: "We do not know."

Jesus says:

> "Neither will I tell you by what authority I do these things."

This is not evasion. It is exposure. **The Servant Son will not throw pearls before those who have already refused the witness God sent them.** The same heart that rejects John will not receive the testimony of the Christ.

The week is moving toward the cross. The King has entered. The temple has been cleansed. The leaders have set themselves against Him. The hour is at hand.

---

## Closing prayer

Father, You sent Your Servant Son into the city of David — humble, mounted on a colt, weeping over Jerusalem, cleansing Your house. Cleanse the temple of our hearts. Drive out the merchants and tables that crowd out prayer. Curse the leafy display that bears no fruit. And teach us to pray with faith and a forgiving heart, so that mountains move at the word of those who walk with Your Son. In Jesus' name, amen.
