# The Servant Son of God: The Great Reckoning

Mark 12 is the Servant Son in the temple, fielding question after question — and turning each one into a judgment on those who asked.

He tells a parable that exposes the leaders as murderers of the heir. He silences the Pharisees on taxes, the Sadducees on the resurrection, and the scribes on the greatest commandment. He warns of religion that loves long robes and despises widows. And He honors a widow no one else noticed.

This is the Servant pronouncing the last public verdict on the religious establishment before the cross.

## The parable of the tenants

He speaks to the chief priests and elders in a parable:

A man planted a vineyard, leased it to tenants, and went into another country. When the time came, he sent a servant to receive some fruit. The tenants beat him and sent him away empty-handed. He sent another. They struck him on the head and treated him shamefully. He sent another. They killed him. Many others — some they beat, some they killed.

Then the owner had one beloved son. He sent him last of all, saying:

> "They will respect my son."

But the tenants said to one another: "This is the heir. Come, let us kill him, and the inheritance will be ours." They killed him and threw him out of the vineyard.

Jesus asks:

> "What will the owner of the vineyard do? He will come and destroy the tenants and give the vineyard to others."

He concludes with Psalm 118:

> "The stone that the builders rejected has become the cornerstone."

The leaders perceive that He has told the parable against them. They want to arrest Him — but they fear the people. So they leave Him and go away.

**The Servant Son tells them, to their faces, that they are about to murder the Son of God.** And the parable becomes prophecy within the week.

## A trap about taxes

They send some Pharisees and Herodians to trap Him in His words:

> "Is it lawful to pay taxes to Caesar, or not?"

If He says yes, He looks like a collaborator. If He says no, He is a rebel.

He asks for a denarius:

> "Whose likeness and inscription is this?"

They answer: "Caesar's."

Jesus says:

> "Render to Caesar the things that are Caesar's, and to God the things that are God's."

They marvel at Him.

The coin bears Caesar's image — give it back to him. But what bears God's image? You do. **Give God the whole of yourself — for you bear His image.** Caesar gets coins. God gets souls.

## The Sadducees on resurrection

The Sadducees, who say there is no resurrection, ask Him about a hypothetical: a woman married to seven brothers in succession. Whose wife will she be in the resurrection?

Jesus answers:

> "Is this not the reason you are wrong, because you know neither the Scriptures nor the power of God?"

In the resurrection people neither marry nor are given in marriage. They are like angels in heaven.

Then He goes deeper. He quotes from Exodus, where God says to Moses at the bush:

> "I am the God of Abraham, and the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob."

He adds:

> "He is not God of the dead, but of the living. You are quite wrong."

The God of the living does not say *I was* their God. He says *I am*. Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob are alive to God. **The resurrection is not a footnote. It is the consequence of God's eternal covenant with His people.**

## The greatest commandment

A scribe — one of the few honest seekers in the chapter — asks:

> "Which commandment is the most important of all?"

Jesus answers with the Shema and Leviticus 19 in one breath:

> "Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one. And you shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind and with all your strength. The second is this: You shall love your neighbor as yourself. There is no other commandment greater than these."

The scribe agrees beautifully. He sees that to love God and neighbor is more than all burnt offerings and sacrifices.

Jesus sees that he has answered wisely:

> "You are not far from the kingdom of God."

*Not far* — not yet inside. The honest seeker is closer than the hypocrite, but he must still come the rest of the way. The way in is the Servant Son.

## David's Son and David's Lord

While teaching in the temple, Jesus poses His own question:

> "How can the scribes say that the Christ is the son of David? David himself, in the Holy Spirit, declared, 'The Lord said to my Lord, "Sit at my right hand, until I put your enemies under your feet."' David himself calls him Lord. So how is he his son?"

The Christ is both David's descendant and David's Lord. He is the Son of David — and infinitely more.

**The Servant Son is not merely a Jewish messianic candidate. He is the Lord that David himself adored.** The crowd hears Him gladly.

## Beware of the scribes

He warns:

> "Beware of the scribes, who like to walk around in long robes and like greetings in the marketplaces and the best seats in the synagogues and the places of honor at feasts, who devour widows' houses and for a pretense make long prayers. They will receive the greater condemnation."

This is religion as costume. Religion as status. Religion that uses the vulnerable as raw material. The Servant Son condemns it without mincing a word.

## The widow's two coins

He sits down opposite the treasury and watches the crowd putting money in the offering box. Many rich people put in large sums.

A poor widow comes and puts in two small copper coins — together worth a fraction of a penny.

He calls His disciples:

> "Truly, I say to you, this poor widow has put in more than all those who are contributing to the offering box. For they all contributed out of their abundance, but she out of her poverty has put in everything she had, all she had to live on."

**The Servant Son does not measure giving by amount. He measures it by cost.** What looks small to the world looks great to heaven when it represents everything you had.

The chapter began with leaders who would murder the Son for the vineyard. It ends with a widow who gave everything she had for the Lord of the temple. The contrast is the verdict.

---

## Closing prayer

Father, give us hearts that render to Caesar the coins and render to You the whole of ourselves, since we bear Your image. Save us from religion that loves long robes and devours widows' houses. Give us the simple devotion of the widow with two coins — willing to give everything we have, because we have come to know the Servant Son who gave everything for us. In Jesus' name, amen.
