# The Servant Son of God: Rejected at Home, Provider for the Crowd

Mark 6 holds a sharp contrast.

In His own hometown, Jesus is despised. The people who watched Him grow up cannot see who He has always been.

A few verses later, He multiplies five loaves and two fish to feed five thousand. He walks on the sea. He stretches out His hand to a sinking Peter.

The Servant Son is rejected by His own and yet keeps serving. Unbelief does not stop His mercy. Familiarity does not exhaust His glory.

## A prophet without honor

Jesus comes to His hometown of Nazareth. He teaches in the synagogue. The people are astonished — but in the wrong way:

> "Is not this the carpenter, the son of Mary and brother of James and Joses and Judas and Simon?"

They know His family. They know His trade. And because they think they know Him, they cannot see Him.

They take offense at Him.

Jesus says:

> "A prophet is not without honor, except in his hometown and among his relatives and in his own household."

Mark gives us a sobering line: He could do no mighty work there, except heal a few sick people. He marvels at their unbelief.

**Familiarity without faith blinds us to the Servant Son standing in our midst.** The most dangerous distance from Christ is when He has been near our whole life and we never truly saw Him.

## Sending out the twelve

Jesus sends the twelve out two by two with authority over unclean spirits. He gives them simple instructions — take no bread, no bag, no money. Wear sandals. Do not move from house to house. If a town will not receive you, shake the dust off your feet.

They go out. They preach repentance. They cast out demons. They anoint the sick with oil and heal them.

The Servant Son does not keep the ministry to Himself. He extends it through ordinary men sent out in His authority.

## The death of John the Baptist

Mark pauses to tell the story of John's death. Herod had imprisoned John for confronting his adulterous marriage to Herodias. Herodias hated John and waited for her moment.

At a birthday banquet, Herodias's daughter dances. Herod is pleased and swears an oath: ask whatever you wish, up to half my kingdom. Her mother tells her what to ask. She returns:

> "Bring me at once the head of John the Baptist on a platter."

Herod is sorry — but for the sake of his oaths and his guests, he does it.

This is the way of fallen kings: pride bound to oath, fear of losing face, willing to murder the prophet to keep the dinner pleasant.

**The way of the Servant Son could not be more different.** Herod murders to preserve his honor. Jesus dies to save His enemies.

## Five loaves, two fish

The apostles return and tell Jesus all they have done. He takes them away to a desolate place to rest.

But the crowd sees them going and runs ahead on foot. When Jesus comes ashore, He sees a great crowd. He has compassion on them:

> "They were like sheep without a shepherd."

He begins to teach them many things.

As evening comes, the disciples urge Him to send the people away to buy food. Jesus answers: "You give them something to eat."

They protest: it would cost two hundred denarii.

He asks: "How many loaves do you have? Go and see."

Five loaves. Two fish.

## "He looked up to heaven"

He commands them to sit on the green grass in groups of hundreds and fifties. He takes the bread.

> "He looked up to heaven and said a blessing and broke the loaves and gave them to the disciples to set before the people."

Everyone eats. Everyone is satisfied. They gather twelve baskets of leftovers.

Five thousand men — plus women and children — fed from five loaves and two fish.

**The Servant Son does not feed by His own resources. He looks up to heaven and blesses what the Father has given.** He gives the broken bread into the hands of His disciples — and they distribute it to the crowd.

This is the pattern of the gospel and of all ministry: looking up, blessing, breaking, giving. The Servant feeds the world through the hands of those He sends.

## Walking on the sea

He makes the disciples get into the boat and go to the other side, while He dismisses the crowd. He goes up on the mountain to pray.

In the evening, the boat is out on the sea and He is alone on land. He sees them straining at the oars, the wind against them.

About the fourth watch of the night — between three and six in the morning — He comes to them, walking on the sea.

He meant to pass by them.

They see Him and cry out, thinking it is a ghost. He speaks immediately:

> "Take heart; it is I. Do not be afraid."

He gets into the boat. The wind ceases. They are utterly astounded — because they had not understood about the loaves. Their hearts were hardened.

## The fringe of His garment

They land at Gennesaret. People recognize Him and run through the whole region, bringing the sick on beds. Wherever He goes — villages, cities, countryside — they lay the sick in the marketplaces and beg Him that they might touch even the fringe of His garment.

> "As many as touched it were made well."

The Servant Son rejected at Nazareth becomes the healer of multitudes elsewhere. Where one town shuts the door of unbelief, another runs to Him with arms full of need.

---

## Closing prayer

Father, save us from the familiarity that hardens our hearts to the Servant Son among us. Let us see Him as He is — the Shepherd who has compassion on the sheep, the Bread who feeds the crowd, the Lord who walks on every storm and says, "Take heart; it is I." Multiply what little we have and give us hands to distribute Your mercy to a hungry world. In Jesus' name, amen.
