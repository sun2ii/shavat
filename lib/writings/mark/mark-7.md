# The Servant Son of God: True Defilement and Surprising Faith

Mark 7 is a chapter about cleanness — and where it really comes from.

The religious leaders are obsessed with hands. Jesus shows them the heart.

The Pharisees draw lines between Jew and Gentile, clean and unclean. Jesus crosses those lines to heal a Gentile woman's daughter and to give hearing and speech to a deaf man.

The Servant Son redefines what defiles, and extends mercy where no Pharisee would have looked.

## Tradition vs. command

The Pharisees and scribes come from Jerusalem and notice that some of Jesus' disciples eat with unwashed hands. They demand an explanation:

> "Why do your disciples not walk according to the tradition of the elders, but eat with defiled hands?"

This is not the Law of Moses. This is the *tradition of the elders* — the layer of human regulation that had grown up around the Law over centuries.

Jesus answers them with Isaiah:

> "This people honors me with their lips, but their heart is far from me. In vain do they worship me, teaching as doctrines the commandments of men."

Then He delivers a stinging diagnosis:

> "You leave the commandment of God and hold to the tradition of men."

## "Corban" — religion that excuses disobedience

He gives an example. The Law commands honor of father and mother. But the Pharisees had developed a rule: if a man declared his resources "Corban" — devoted to God — he could refuse to use them to help his aging parents.

Religion had invented a loophole around the Fifth Commandment.

> "Thus making void the word of God by your tradition that you have handed down."

**Any religious system that lets you disobey God in the name of God is not faith. It is fraud.** The Servant Son tears off the mask.

## Nothing outside defiles — but what comes out

Jesus calls the people to Him:

> "There is nothing outside a person that by going into him can defile him, but the things that come out of a person are what defile him."

Mark adds an editorial note that lands like a thunderclap: "Thus he declared all foods clean."

The Servant Son is rewriting the food laws on His own authority — because He is the One who gave them, and He is bringing the new covenant.

In private the disciples ask. He explains:

> "From within, out of the heart of man, come evil thoughts, sexual immorality, theft, murder, adultery, coveting, wickedness, deceit, sensuality, envy, slander, pride, foolishness. All these evil things come from within, and they defile a person."

This is the deep diagnosis. Sin is not a soap-and-water problem. The heart itself is unclean, and only Christ can wash it.

**No external cleansing reaches the inner corruption. The Servant Son does what no ritual can do — He cleanses the heart.**

## A Syrophoenician woman's faith

Jesus leaves and goes to the region of Tyre and Sidon — Gentile country. He enters a house and wants no one to know He is there.

But He cannot be hidden.

A woman whose little daughter has an unclean spirit hears of Him. She is a Greek, a Syrophoenician by birth. She falls at His feet and begs Him to cast the demon out.

Jesus answers with what sounds like a hard saying:

> "Let the children be fed first, for it is not right to take the children's bread and throw it to the dogs."

The "children" are Israel. The "dogs" is a softened Greek diminutive — household puppies, not feral curs. He is testing her faith with the apparent order of His mission: to Israel first.

## "Yes, Lord — yet even the dogs"

She does not get offended. She does not despair. She turns His own picture into a plea:

> "Yes, Lord; yet even the dogs under the table eat the children's crumbs."

This is one of the most astonishing answers in the Gospels. She accepts the picture. She does not demand the children's bread. She asks only for the crumbs that fall.

Jesus responds:

> "For this statement you may go your way; the demon has left your daughter."

She goes home and finds the child lying in bed, the demon gone.

**The Servant Son is moved by faith that humbles itself and refuses to let go.** A Gentile woman receives what proud Israel rejected.

## Ephphatha — be opened

Jesus returns to the Sea of Galilee through the Decapolis. They bring Him a deaf man who has a speech impediment.

He takes the man aside, away from the crowd. He puts His fingers into the man's ears. He spits and touches his tongue. He looks up to heaven, sighs, and says:

> "Ephphatha" — "Be opened."

Immediately the man's ears are opened. The tongue is released. He speaks plainly.

Jesus charges them to tell no one — but the more He charges them, the more they proclaim it. They are astonished beyond measure:

> "He has done all things well. He even makes the deaf hear and the mute speak."

**The Servant Son opens what has been closed.** Deaf ears. Mute tongues. Hardened hearts. The Gentile and the Jew. He stretches out His hand and says *Ephphatha*, and what was closed is opened.

## Closing prayer

Father, save us from religion that washes our hands and leaves our hearts unwashed. Show us the corruption that comes from within, and cleanse us at the deep place that only Christ can reach. Give us the humble persistence of the Syrophoenician woman, and the open ears and open mouths that the Servant Son grants by His Word. In Jesus' name, amen.
