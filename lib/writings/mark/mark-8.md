# The Servant Son of God: "Who Do You Say I Am?"

Mark 8 is the hinge of the Gospel.

Up to this point, the Servant has been revealing Himself through deeds — healings, exorcisms, feeding crowds, calming storms. The question hovering over every chapter has been: *Who is this?*

In Mark 8, the question is finally answered. Peter says, "You are the Christ." And from that moment, the Servant turns His face toward Jerusalem and begins to teach openly that He must suffer, be killed, and rise again.

The Servant is the Christ. And the Christ is the Crucified.

## Feeding four thousand

He has compassion on a crowd that has been with Him three days and has nothing to eat. The disciples — who saw the five thousand fed just chapters ago — ask:

> "How can one feed these people with bread here in this desolate place?"

They have already forgotten.

Jesus asks: "How many loaves do you have?"

Seven.

He commands the crowd to sit down. He takes the seven loaves, gives thanks, breaks them, and gives them to the disciples to set before the people. He also has a few small fish, and He blesses them.

About four thousand people eat. They take up seven baskets of leftovers.

**The Servant feeds the crowd a second time — not because the first wasn't enough, but because the disciples weren't yet ready to learn from it.** He keeps teaching the same lesson until we hear it.

## The leaven of the Pharisees

In the boat, the disciples have forgotten to bring bread. They have only one loaf.

Jesus warns them: "Watch out; beware of the leaven of the Pharisees and the leaven of Herod."

They think He is rebuking them for forgetting bread.

He sighs:

> "Why are you discussing the fact that you have no bread? Do you not yet perceive or understand? Are your hearts hardened? Having eyes do you not see, and having ears do you not hear?"

Then He reminds them of the five thousand and the four thousand. How many baskets? Twelve. Seven.

> "Do you not yet understand?"

**The Servant has fed them with His own hand, and yet they still worry about bread.** The danger of unbelief is not just out there in the Pharisees. It is in the disciples' own hearts.

## A blind man healed in stages

At Bethsaida, they bring a blind man to Jesus. He takes him by the hand and leads him out of the village.

He spits on the man's eyes, lays His hands on him, and asks: "Do you see anything?"

The man looks up and says:

> "I see men, but they look like trees, walking."

He sees, but not clearly. So Jesus lays His hands on his eyes again. He looks intently. Everything is restored. He sees everything clearly.

This is the only two-stage healing in the Gospels. Mark places it here for a reason: it is a parable of what is about to happen to the disciples.

They are beginning to see. But not yet clearly. The Servant Son is going to lay His hands on them a second time — and they will see who He really is.

## "Who do you say I am?"

Jesus goes with the disciples to the villages of Caesarea Philippi. On the way He asks:

> "Who do people say that I am?"

They report what the crowds say: John the Baptist, Elijah, one of the prophets.

Then He asks the question that every soul must answer:

> "But who do you say that I am?"

Peter answers:

> "You are the Christ."

This is the confession. The whole Gospel of Mark has been climbing to it.

**Every other identification of Jesus falls short. Only "You are the Christ" hits the target.** And not Christ as the crowd imagined — a political deliverer to throw off Rome. Christ as God will define Him: the Suffering Servant.

## The first prediction of the cross

From that moment on, Jesus begins to teach plainly what He has hinted at darkly:

> "The Son of Man must suffer many things and be rejected by the elders and the chief priests and the scribes and be killed, and after three days rise again."

He said this plainly.

Peter takes Him aside and rebukes Him. The Christ cannot suffer. The Christ cannot die.

Jesus turns and sees the disciples. He rebukes Peter — in front of them all:

> "Get behind me, Satan! For you are not setting your mind on the things of God, but on the things of man."

Peter just confessed Christ. Now Peter is rebuked as Satan. The same lips can speak truth and temptation. Loving the Christ without loving His cross is the oldest temptation in the world.

## Take up your cross and follow

Jesus calls the crowd along with the disciples. He gives the great paradox:

> "If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow me. For whoever would save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for my sake and the gospel's will save it."

To follow the Servant Son is to carry a cross. Not a metaphor for inconvenience — a cross. The instrument of death.

> "For what does it profit a man to gain the whole world and forfeit his soul?"

There is no exchange you can make for your soul. You cannot trade enough of the world to buy it back.

The Christ is the Crucified — and so are His followers. The Servant Son leads a people who lay down their lives to find them.

---

## Closing prayer

Father, open our eyes a second time so that we see Christ clearly — not as the crowd imagines Him, but as Peter confessed Him and as You revealed Him: the suffering, dying, rising Christ. Save us from the leaven of unbelief. Give us courage to take up the cross and follow the Servant Son — losing our lives for His sake and finding them in Him alone. In Jesus' name, amen.
