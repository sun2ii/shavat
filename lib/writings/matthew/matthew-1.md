# The Promised King: A Royal Genealogy

Matthew does not begin with a manger.

Matthew begins with a family tree.

This is no accident. Matthew is writing first to a Jewish audience, and for the Jewish mind, the most important question about any claimant to the throne of David is one: *Is he in the line?*

> "The book of the genealogy of Jesus Christ, the son of David, the son of Abraham."

Two names anchor the whole tree. *Son of David* — the King. *Son of Abraham* — the heir of the promise. Every promise God ever made to Israel narrows to one Man at the bottom of this list.

## A book of names

The genealogy runs for sixteen verses. Most modern readers want to skip it. Matthew put it first because he could not say what he needed to say without it.

He arranges the list in three sets of fourteen generations:

- Abraham to David
- David to the exile
- The exile to the Christ

Fourteen — twice the number of completion. Three sets — the rhythm of fulfillment. This is no random census. This is history shaped by God's hand.

**The Promised King did not arrive randomly.** He arrived at the appointed time, in the appointed line, born of the appointed mother.

## Surprising names

The genealogy is not what a king's defender would have invented.

Tamar — who tricked Judah into fathering her sons.
Rahab — a Gentile prostitute from Jericho.
Ruth — a Moabite widow from outside the covenant.
The wife of Uriah — Bathsheba, taken in adultery by David.

Four women in the line of the Promised King. Four women whose stories embarrass the family.

Matthew puts them in deliberately. He is telling us at the start: this King has come from a line of broken people. He has come for broken people. He has come to redeem the whole story — the gaps, the failures, the scandals — and to weave them into the great work of salvation.

**The Promised King is not embarrassed by His ancestors.** He owns them. He came to redeem them. He came to redeem us.

## The line continues

The names roll on. Patriarchs. Kings. Faithful and unfaithful. Some great, some forgettable. Abraham. Jacob. David. Solomon. Hezekiah. Josiah. And then names many have never heard — Eliakim, Azor, Zadok, Achim — the obscure forefathers who carried the promise through the centuries even when no one was watching.

The line did not break. Even through Babylon. Even through exile. Even through the silent centuries between Malachi and Matthew. God's promise to David held.

> "Joseph the husband of Mary, of whom Jesus was born, who is called Christ."

The list arrives at Joseph. But notice the careful change in language. Of every other man it has been said *X was the father of Y*. Of Joseph it says: *Joseph the husband of Mary, of whom Jesus was born.*

**The Promised King is in the royal line by adoption through Joseph — but His Father is God Himself.**

## The angel speaks to Joseph

> "Now the birth of Jesus Christ took place in this way."

Mary is found to be with child from the Holy Spirit before she and Joseph come together. Joseph, being a just man, resolves to divorce her quietly.

But as he considers these things, an angel of the Lord appears to him in a dream:

> "Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as your wife, for that which is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit. She will bear a son, and you shall call his name Jesus, for he will save his people from their sins."

*Joseph, son of David.* The angel addresses him by his royal title. Joseph stands in the line of the kings — and through him, the child will be legally heir to David's throne.

The name is given: *Jesus* — *the LORD saves.* And the mission is given: *he will save his people from their sins.*

**The Promised King does not come for political deliverance first. He comes for the deeper bondage — sin itself.**

## "Immanuel"

Matthew tells us this took place to fulfill what the Lord had spoken by the prophet:

> "Behold, the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and they shall call his name Immanuel" — which means, "God with us."

Isaiah spoke seven centuries earlier. The promise waited. The promise was kept.

The Promised King is *Immanuel*. Not a king visiting His subjects from a safe distance, but the very presence of God among His people.

When you read the rest of Matthew — the Sermon on the Mount, the parables, the miracles, the passion, the resurrection — you are reading the unfolding of *Immanuel.* God is not far off. God is with us. In a man. In a King. In Jesus.

## Joseph obeys

> "When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he took his wife, but knew her not until she had given birth to a son. And he called his name Jesus."

Joseph is quiet in the Gospels. He says nothing — he only does.

**The first man to act in the new covenant is a quiet carpenter who obeyed an angel without argument.** He took Mary. He took the Child. He gave Him a name.

The Promised King has arrived. The line is complete. The throne of David has its rightful heir. Heaven and earth are now joined in one Person, born of Mary, named Jesus, called Immanuel.

---

## Closing prayer

Father, thank You for the unbroken line through Abraham, through David, through exile, through silence, until at last Jesus Christ was born — the rightful King in David's line, born of a virgin, called *God with us.* Thank You that He came to save His people from their sins. Make us, like Joseph, quick to obey what You have spoken — and let us welcome the Promised King into the home of our hearts. In Jesus' name, amen.
