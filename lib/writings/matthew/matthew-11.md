# The Promised King: Come to Me, and I Will Give You Rest

Matthew 11 ends with one of the most beloved invitations in the Bible:

> "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest."

But getting there, the chapter passes through some dark valleys.

John the Baptist sends from prison, asking whether Jesus is really the One.
The cities of Galilee, despite seeing the King's mightiest works, refuse to repent.
The King pronounces woes on Chorazin, Bethsaida, and Capernaum.

And in the middle of it all, Jesus thanks the Father for hiding the Kingdom from the wise and revealing it to little children.

The Promised King invites the weary — but the weary are the ones who admit they are weary. **Rest is not for the self-sufficient.**

## John's question

Now when John hears in prison about the deeds of the Christ, he sends word by his disciples:

> "Are you the one who is to come, or shall we look for another?"

Even John doubts.

He had baptized Jesus. He had heard the Father's voice. He had seen the dove descend. He had announced Him to Israel.

But now John is in prison. The mission seems delayed. The Messiah he announced has not yet thrown down Herod, freed Israel, or established the Kingdom. From his dark cell, even John wonders.

**The Promised King does not condemn the question.** He answers it.

> "Go and tell John what you hear and see: the blind receive their sight and the lame walk, lepers are cleansed and the deaf hear, and the dead are raised up, and the poor have good news preached to them."

These are the marks Isaiah said would identify the Messiah. Jesus is doing them. The answer is yes — but it requires reading the Scriptures and reading the works together.

He adds:

> "And blessed is the one who is not offended by me."

Some are offended that He has not come as a political deliverer. Blessed are those whose faith is not shaken when the King turns out to be different from their expectations.

## Praise for John

When John's disciples leave, Jesus speaks to the crowds about him:

> "What did you go out into the wilderness to see? A reed shaken by the wind?"

John was not soft. He was not blown by the winds of opinion.

> "What then did you go out to see? A man dressed in soft clothing? Behold, those who wear soft clothing are in kings' houses."

John lived in the wilderness, not the palace.

> "What then did you go out to see? A prophet? Yes, I tell you, and more than a prophet. This is he of whom it is written, 'Behold, I send my messenger before your face, who will prepare your way before you.'"

> "Truly, I say to you, among those born of women there has arisen no one greater than John the Baptist."

This is high praise. And then:

> "Yet the one who is least in the kingdom of heaven is greater than he."

John stood on the threshold of the Kingdom. He pointed to it. He prepared for it. But he died before its full inauguration in the death and resurrection of Christ. The smallest disciple after the cross stands in a privileged place John could only foresee.

## "From the days of John"

> "From the days of John the Baptist until now the kingdom of heaven has suffered violence, and the violent take it by force."

The Kingdom is advancing in spite of opposition. It is also being received by people willing to break with everything — willing to seize it with both hands.

## "We played the flute"

But the generation that watched John and Jesus would not be satisfied with either:

> "But to what shall I compare this generation? It is like children sitting in the marketplaces and calling to their playmates, 'We played the flute for you, and you did not dance; we sang a dirge, and you did not mourn.' For John came neither eating nor drinking, and they say, 'He has a demon.' The Son of Man came eating and drinking, and they say, 'Look at him! A glutton and a drunkard, a friend of tax collectors and sinners!'"

John was too strict. Jesus was too welcoming. Neither could please them. **The Promised King exposes the heart of unbelief — it cannot be pleased.** It will always find a reason to reject.

> "Yet wisdom is justified by her deeds."

The wisdom of God is vindicated by what it produces — and what it produces in those who actually receive it is unmistakable.

## Woes on Galilean cities

Then He begins to denounce the cities where most of His mighty works had been done, because they did not repent:

> "Woe to you, Chorazin! Woe to you, Bethsaida! For if the mighty works done in you had been done in Tyre and Sidon, they would have repented long ago in sackcloth and ashes."

The pagan cities of Tyre and Sidon would have repented at less than what these Galilean towns have seen. But the religious privilege of the Galilean towns has produced indifference.

> "And you, Capernaum, will you be exalted to heaven? You will be brought down to Hades. For if the mighty works done in you had been done in Sodom, it would have remained until this day."

**The greater the light, the greater the responsibility.** Capernaum had seen more of the King's power than any other place on earth. Their refusal to repent put them under a heavier judgment than Sodom.

## Hidden from the wise

At that time Jesus declares:

> "I thank you, Father, Lord of heaven and earth, that you have hidden these things from the wise and understanding and revealed them to little children; yes, Father, for such was your gracious will."

This is one of the most stunning prayers in the Gospels. The King thanks the Father for the very sovereignty that hides the Kingdom from the proud and reveals it to the humble.

The pattern is not new. Throughout the Bible, God has chosen the unlikely, the small, the despised. The proud cannot enter the Kingdom because their pride blocks their sight.

> "All things have been handed over to me by my Father, and no one knows the Son except the Father, and no one knows the Father except the Son and anyone to whom the Son chooses to reveal him."

The Promised King is the unique Revealer of the Father. There is no knowing God apart from Him.

## "Come to me"

Then comes one of the most beautiful invitations in all of Scripture:

> "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest."

He addresses the weary. The burdened. Those crushed under the weight of religion, of failure, of sin, of life.

He does not say *come to a system.* He does not say *come to a doctrine.* He says *come to me.*

> "Take my yoke upon you, and learn from me, for I am gentle and lowly in heart, and you will find rest for your souls. For my yoke is easy, and my burden is light."

A yoke is for working. He does not promise the absence of labor. He promises that the yoke worn beside Him will be easy, the burden light. He shares the load. He sets the pace. **The Promised King is gentle and lowly in heart — and that is why those who come to Him find rest.**

This is the invitation that closes the chapter. The Pharisees would not come. Many of the cities would not come. But the weary — those who knew they were weary — could come. They still can.

---

## Closing prayer

Father, thank You for hiding these things from the wise and revealing them to little children. Make us little. Make us willing to come weary and laden, with nothing but our need. We hear the Promised King's voice — *come to me* — and we come. Give us rest for our souls. Yoke us beside Him, and teach us His gentleness and lowliness of heart. In Jesus' name, amen.
