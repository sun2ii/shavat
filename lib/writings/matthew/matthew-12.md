# The Promised King: Lord of the Sabbath

Matthew 12 brings the King's confrontation with the religious establishment to a critical heat.

Two Sabbath controversies open the chapter — the disciples picking grain, and the man with a withered hand healed in the synagogue. The Pharisees plot to destroy Him. He withdraws, but the crowds follow. He heals a demon-possessed man, and the Pharisees accuse Him of working by the prince of demons. He warns of the unforgivable sin. He refuses to give them a sign except the sign of Jonah. And He redefines His family — *whoever does the will of my Father in heaven is my brother and sister and mother.*

The Promised King will not bow to religion that strangles compassion, and He will not negotiate with hearts that call light darkness.

## Lord of the Sabbath

At that time Jesus goes through the grainfields on a Sabbath. His disciples are hungry, and they begin to pluck heads of grain and to eat.

The Pharisees say to Him: *Look, your disciples are doing what is not lawful to do on the Sabbath.*

(Plucking grain to eat was not forbidden by the Law — it was forbidden by the *tradition of the elders* who had stacked rules around the Sabbath.)

Jesus answers:

> "Have you not read what David did when he was hungry, and those who were with him: how he entered the house of God and ate the bread of the Presence, which it was not lawful for him to eat nor for those who were with him, but only for the priests?"

David ate consecrated bread when human need required it. The Pharisees know the story. They know how to read it.

He pushes further:

> "Or have you not read in the Law how on the Sabbath the priests in the temple profane the Sabbath and are guiltless? I tell you, something greater than the temple is here."

He stands greater than the temple. Then He drops the verdict:

> "And if you had known what this means, 'I desire mercy, and not sacrifice,' you would not have condemned the guiltless. For the Son of Man is lord of the Sabbath."

**The Promised King declares Himself Lord of the Sabbath.** The day belongs to Him. He defines what is lawful on it.

## A man with a withered hand

He goes on from there and enters their synagogue. A man is there with a withered hand. They ask Him, hoping to accuse Him: *Is it lawful to heal on the Sabbath?*

He answers:

> "Which one of you who has a sheep, if it falls into a pit on the Sabbath, will not take hold of it and lift it out? Of how much more value is a man than a sheep! So it is lawful to do good on the Sabbath."

Then He says to the man: *Stretch out your hand.* The man stretches it out, and it is restored, healthy like the other.

The Pharisees go out and conspire against Him, how to destroy Him.

**The Pharisees would rather kill the King than let Him heal on the Sabbath.** Their religion has become so rigid that mercy itself has become an offense.

## The Servant of the Lord

Jesus, aware of this, withdraws from there. Many follow Him, and He heals them all. He orders them not to make Him known. This fulfills Isaiah:

> "Behold, my servant whom I have chosen, my beloved with whom my soul is well pleased. I will put my Spirit upon him, and he will proclaim justice to the Gentiles. He will not quarrel or cry aloud, nor will anyone hear his voice in the streets; a bruised reed he will not break, and a smoldering wick he will not quench, until he brings justice to victory; and in his name the Gentiles will hope."

Matthew adds this Old Testament quote to show that the King's quiet, gentle ministry — withdrawing rather than confronting, healing in obscurity rather than seeking publicity — is the very pattern Isaiah foretold.

**The Promised King is also the Promised Servant.** He will not break the bruised reed. He will not snuff out the smoldering wick. The weakest faith is safe with Him.

## Beelzebul accusation

Then a demon-oppressed man who is blind and mute is brought to Him. He heals him, so that the man speaks and sees. All the people are amazed:

> "Can this be the Son of David?"

But when the Pharisees hear it, they say:

> "It is only by Beelzebul, the prince of demons, that this man casts out demons."

Knowing their thoughts, Jesus answers with simple logic:

> "Every kingdom divided against itself is laid waste, and no city or house divided against itself will stand. And if Satan casts out Satan, he is divided against himself. How then will his kingdom stand?"

If His casting out demons is by Beelzebul, then by whom do their sons cast them out? They will be their judges.

> "But if it is by the Spirit of God that I cast out demons, then the kingdom of God has come upon you."

> "How can someone enter a strong man's house and plunder his goods, unless he first binds the strong man? Then indeed he may plunder his house."

**The Promised King has bound the strong man.** He has invaded Satan's territory and is taking back what was stolen.

## The unforgivable sin

> "Therefore I tell you, every sin and blasphemy will be forgiven people, but the blasphemy against the Spirit will not be forgiven. And whoever speaks a word against the Son of Man will be forgiven, but whoever speaks against the Holy Spirit will not be forgiven, either in this age or in the age to come."

The Pharisees are calling the work of the Holy Spirit the work of Satan. They are calling light darkness, and good evil. To persist in that — to know it is the work of God and call it the work of the devil — is to refuse the only saving witness.

**Those troubled by this warning are not the ones who have committed it.** Those who fear they have are showing the soft heart that proves they have not. The ones who actually commit this sin do not care.

## Words and the heart

> "Either make the tree good and its fruit good, or make the tree bad and its fruit bad, for the tree is known by its fruit."

He turns to them sharply:

> "You brood of vipers! How can you speak good, when you are evil? For out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks."

> "I tell you, on the day of judgment people will give account for every careless word they speak, for by your words you will be justified, and by your words you will be condemned."

**The mouth reveals the heart.** The Promised King takes our words seriously — because they tell the truth about who we are inside.

## The sign of Jonah

Then some of the scribes and Pharisees say: *Teacher, we wish to see a sign from you.*

He answers:

> "An evil and adulterous generation seeks for a sign, but no sign will be given to it except the sign of the prophet Jonah. For just as Jonah was three days and three nights in the belly of the great fish, so will the Son of Man be three days and three nights in the heart of the earth."

The sign He gives them is His own death and resurrection. The cross and the empty tomb are the only sign sufficient — and the only sign He will give.

> "The men of Nineveh will rise up at the judgment with this generation and condemn it, for they repented at the preaching of Jonah, and behold, something greater than Jonah is here."

A pagan city judged the religious generation. A pagan queen judged the religious leaders. **The Promised King says that smaller light produced more repentance in foreigners than the greatest Light has produced in His own people.**

## The empty house

He gives a warning about an unclean spirit that leaves a person and then returns with seven other spirits more evil than itself. The last state of that person is worse than the first.

> "So also will it be with this evil generation."

Moral reform without the King is dangerous. An empty house is open to occupation. **Only when the King fills the house is the soul truly safe.**

## His true family

While He is still speaking to the people, His mother and His brothers stand outside, asking to speak to Him. Someone tells Him.

He replies:

> "Who is my mother, and who are my brothers?"

Stretching out His hand toward His disciples, He says:

> "Here are my mother and my brothers! For whoever does the will of my Father in heaven is my brother and sister and mother."

**The Promised King redefines family.** Not by blood. Not by ethnicity. By the will of the Father — that is, by responding to the Son.

---

## Closing prayer

Father, thank You that the Promised King is Lord of the Sabbath, gentle with the bruised reed, and merciful with the smoldering wick. Save us from religion that strangles compassion. Save us from the hardness that calls light darkness. Make our hearts good, so our words may be good — and let us be among those who do the will of our Father in heaven, brothers and sisters of the King. In Jesus' name, amen.
