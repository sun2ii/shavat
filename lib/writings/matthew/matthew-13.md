# The Promised King: The Hidden Kingdom

Matthew 13 is the chapter of parables.

Eight parables in one chapter. Each one a window into how the Kingdom of Heaven actually works. The sower. The weeds among the wheat. The mustard seed. The leaven. The hidden treasure. The pearl of great price. The net. And the householder bringing out treasures old and new.

The point is the same in every parable: **the Kingdom of Heaven is not what you expect.** It does not arrive with thrones and armies. It arrives like a seed. Like leaven. Like a hidden treasure. Small. Hidden. But unstoppable.

The Promised King has come. The Kingdom is here. But it is hidden in plain sight — visible only to those who have eyes to see.

## The parable of the sower

That same day Jesus goes out of the house and sits beside the sea. Great crowds gather. He gets into a boat and sits there. The crowd stands on the beach.

He tells them many things in parables, saying:

> "A sower went out to sow. As he sowed, some seeds fell along the path, and the birds came and devoured them. Other seeds fell on rocky ground, where they did not have much soil, and immediately they sprang up, since they had no depth of soil, but when the sun rose they were scorched."

> "Other seeds fell among thorns, and the thorns grew up and choked them. Other seeds fell on good soil and produced grain, some a hundredfold, some sixty, some thirty. He who has ears, let him hear."

## Why parables?

The disciples ask: *Why do you speak to them in parables?*

He answers:

> "To you it has been given to know the secrets of the kingdom of heaven, but to them it has not been given… This is why I speak to them in parables, because seeing they do not see, and hearing they do not hear, nor do they understand."

This fulfills Isaiah:

> "You will indeed hear but never understand, and you will indeed see but never perceive. For this people's heart has grown dull."

Parables reveal to those who lean in and conceal from those who turn away. **They are mercy and judgment in the same words.**

> "But blessed are your eyes, for they see, and your ears, for they hear."

## The sower explained

Jesus interprets the parable Himself. The seed is the Word of the Kingdom.

- **The path** — the hard heart. The evil one snatches the Word away before it can take root.
- **The rocky ground** — the shallow heart. Initial joy, no root, falls away under tribulation.
- **The thorny ground** — the divided heart. The cares of the world and the deceitfulness of riches choke the Word.
- **The good soil** — the receiving heart. Hears, understands, and bears fruit — a hundred, sixty, or thirty times.

**The same seed. The same Sower. The difference is the soil.**

## Weeds among the wheat

He puts another parable before them:

> "The kingdom of heaven may be compared to a man who sowed good seed in his field, but while his men were sleeping, his enemy came and sowed weeds among the wheat and went away."

When the plants come up, the weeds appear too. The servants ask: *Shall we pull them up?* The master says no — pulling the weeds would damage the wheat.

> "Let both grow together until the harvest, and at harvest time I will tell the reapers, 'Gather the weeds first and bind them in bundles to be burned, but gather the wheat into my barn.'"

Later, in private, Jesus explains. The field is the world. The good seed is the children of the Kingdom. The weeds are the children of the evil one. The enemy is the devil. The harvest is the end of the age. The reapers are angels.

**The Kingdom and the kingdom of evil grow side by side in this age.** A final separation is coming. But not yet. Until then, the wheat and the weeds occupy the same field — and the wheat must keep growing despite the weeds around it.

## The mustard seed and the leaven

Two short parables about small beginnings.

> "The kingdom of heaven is like a grain of mustard seed that a man took and sowed in his field. It is the smallest of all seeds, but when it has grown it is larger than all the garden plants and becomes a tree, so that the birds of the air come and make nests in its branches."

> "The kingdom of heaven is like leaven that a woman took and hid in three measures of flour, till it was all leavened."

The Kingdom begins small. It looks like nothing. A baby in Bethlehem. Twelve unlikely men. A crucified Messiah. But it spreads. It grows. It works through everything. **Do not despise small beginnings.**

## The treasure and the pearl

Two parables about the worth of the Kingdom.

> "The kingdom of heaven is like treasure hidden in a field, which a man found and covered up. Then in his joy he goes and sells all that he has and buys that field."

> "Again, the kingdom of heaven is like a merchant in search of fine pearls, who, on finding one pearl of great value, went and sold all that he had and bought it."

In both, the same response: the discoverer sells *everything* to obtain what he found.

**The Kingdom is worth more than anything else you have.** The man who has seen it counts every other treasure as cheap. The merchant who has found the pearl is delighted to give up everything for it.

## The net

> "The kingdom of heaven is like a net that was thrown into the sea and gathered fish of every kind. When it was full, men drew it ashore and sat down and sorted the good into containers but threw away the bad."

So it will be at the end of the age — the angels will separate the evil from the righteous. The wicked will be thrown into the fiery furnace. There will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.

**The Kingdom gathers indiscriminately in this age — and sorts decisively at the end.**

## The new and the old

> "Have you understood all these things?"

They say to Him: *Yes.*

He says:

> "Therefore every scribe who has been trained for the kingdom of heaven is like a master of a house, who brings out of his treasure what is new and what is old."

The disciple trained in the Kingdom holds together the old (the Old Testament) and the new (what the Promised King now reveals). Both are treasure. Neither is discarded.

## Rejected at Nazareth

He comes to His hometown. He teaches them in their synagogue. They are astonished — but in the wrong way:

> "Where did this man get this wisdom and these mighty works? Is not this the carpenter's son? Is not his mother called Mary? And are not his brothers James and Joseph and Simon and Judas?… Where then did this man get all these things?"

They take offense at Him.

Jesus says:

> "A prophet is not without honor except in his hometown and in his own household."

And He did not do many mighty works there, because of their unbelief.

**The same Christ who is the hidden Kingdom for those with eyes to see is invisible to those who think they already know Him too well.** Familiarity without faith blinds.

---

## Closing prayer

Father, give us ears to hear, eyes to see, and good soil hearts to receive Your Word. Let it bear fruit thirty, sixty, a hundredfold. Save us from the hard path, the shallow rock, the choking thorns. Open our eyes to the hidden treasure and the pearl of great price, that we may joyfully sell all to gain Christ. And until the harvest, give us patience to let the wheat and the weeds grow side by side, trusting You to sort them at the end. In Jesus' name, amen.
