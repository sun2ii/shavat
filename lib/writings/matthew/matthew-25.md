# The Promised King: The Final Reckoning

Matthew 25 continues the prophetic discourse with three parables — and then the most vivid picture of the Last Judgment in all of Scripture.

The ten virgins. The talents. The sheep and the goats.

In each parable, the King returns. In each, there is separation — wise from foolish, faithful from wicked, sheep from goats. In each, the verdict is irreversible.

The Promised King is coming back. **And when He comes, everyone will be sorted by what they actually did with the time, the gifts, and the brethren He entrusted to them.**

## The ten virgins

> "Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise. For when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the wise took flasks of oil with their lamps."

As the bridegroom was delayed, they all became drowsy and slept. At midnight there was a cry:

> "Here is the bridegroom! Come out to meet him."

All those virgins rose and trimmed their lamps. The foolish said to the wise: *Give us some of your oil, for our lamps are going out.*

The wise answered: *Since there will not be enough for us and for you, go rather to the dealers and buy for yourselves.*

While they were going to buy, the bridegroom came. Those who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast. The door was shut.

Later the other virgins came: *Lord, lord, open to us.*

He answered: *Truly, I say to you, I do not know you.*

Jesus concludes:

> "Watch therefore, for you know neither the day nor the hour."

**The oil cannot be borrowed at the last moment.** Some preparation cannot be transferred. Faith, real conversion, daily walking with Christ — these cannot be picked up on the way to meet the Bridegroom. The door does shut. The wise enter. The unprepared are left outside.

## The talents

> "For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two, to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away."

He who had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, making five talents more.

He who had received the two talents made two talents more.

But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid his master's money.

After a long time the master came and settled accounts. The first servant said: *Master, you delivered to me five talents; here, I have made five talents more.*

The master said:

> "Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master."

The second servant gave the same kind of report and received the same kind of praise.

Then the one-talent servant came: *Master, I knew you to be a hard man, reaping where you did not sow and gathering where you scattered no seed. So I was afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the ground. Here, you have what is yours.*

His master answered:

> "You wicked and slothful servant! You knew that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I scattered no seed? Then you ought to have invested my money with the bankers, and at my coming I should have received what was my own with interest. So take the talent from him and give it to him who has the ten talents. For to everyone who has will more be given, and he will have abundance. But from the one who has not, even what he has will be taken away. And cast the worthless servant into the outer darkness."

**The Promised King will judge His servants by what they did with what He gave them.** The one-talent servant was not condemned for failing to make five. He was condemned for using *nothing.* For burying what was entrusted to him out of fear of his master.

The faithfulness is measured proportionally — what you do with what you have. The slothfulness is measured absolutely — you did nothing.

## The sheep and the goats

Then comes the most vivid picture of the Last Judgment in all the New Testament.

> "When the Son of Man comes in his glory, and all the angels with him, then he will sit on his glorious throne. Before him will be gathered all the nations, and he will separate people one from another as a shepherd separates the sheep from the goats. And he will place the sheep on his right, but the goats on the left."

Then the King will say to those on His right:

> "Come, you who are blessed by my Father, inherit the kingdom prepared for you from the foundation of the world. For I was hungry and you gave me food, I was thirsty and you gave me drink, I was a stranger and you welcomed me, I was naked and you clothed me, I was sick and you visited me, I was in prison and you came to me."

The righteous answer: *Lord, when did we see you hungry and feed you, or thirsty and give you drink? And when did we see you a stranger and welcome you, or naked and clothe you? And when did we see you sick or in prison and visit you?*

The King will answer:

> "Truly, I say to you, as you did it to one of the least of these my brothers, you did it to me."

Then He will say to those on His left:

> "Depart from me, you cursed, into the eternal fire prepared for the devil and his angels. For I was hungry and you gave me no food, I was thirsty and you gave me no drink, I was a stranger and you did not welcome me, naked and you did not clothe me, sick and in prison and you did not visit me."

They answer: *Lord, when did we see you hungry or thirsty or a stranger or naked or sick or in prison, and did not minister to you?*

He answers:

> "Truly, I say to you, as you did not do it to one of the least of these, you did not do it to me."

> "And these will go away into eternal punishment, but the righteous into eternal life."

## What the parable does and does not say

This parable is not teaching salvation by works. The whole of the New Testament — including Matthew — teaches that we are saved by grace through faith.

What the parable is teaching is that **real faith is shown by real love.** The kind of faith that saves is not a private religious feeling. It expresses itself in compassion for the hungry, the thirsty, the stranger, the naked, the sick, the prisoner.

And the King identifies Himself so closely with the needy — especially with His brothers and sisters in distress — that what is done to them is done to Him.

The sheep did not even know they were ministering to Christ. They simply loved. The goats did not see Christ in the needy either. They simply failed to love. **The presence of Christ in His brethren is exposed by how His followers respond — not by religious claims.**

The verdict is irreversible. The destiny is eternal.

The Promised King will return. The throne will be set. The nations will be gathered. The separation will be made. And the only thing that will matter on that day is whether we knew the King — and whether His life was working through us in love for the least.

## Closing prayer

Father, keep oil in our lamps. Make us faithful with the talents You have entrusted. Make us sheep, not goats — those whose hearts run toward the hungry, the thirsty, the stranger, the naked, the sick, the prisoner — because they bear the face of Your Son. And when the Promised King returns to sit on His glorious throne, may we hear the words: *Come, you who are blessed by my Father, inherit the kingdom prepared for you.* In Jesus' name, amen.
