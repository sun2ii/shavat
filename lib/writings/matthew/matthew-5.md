# The Promised King: The Constitution of the Kingdom

Matthew 5 begins the most famous sermon ever preached.

The Promised King has called His first disciples. He has begun to heal and to teach. Now He climbs a mountain — the way Moses climbed Sinai — and seats Himself as the King. From that seat He gives the constitution of His Kingdom.

It begins with eight beatitudes — declarations of blessing on the kind of people the world does not call blessed. It continues with the great call to be salt and light. And it climbs to a stunning claim: the Promised King has not come to abolish the Law and the Prophets but to *fulfill* them. He raises the standard. He goes beneath the action to the heart.

This is not motivational teaching. This is the Kingdom revealed.

## Up the mountain

> "Seeing the crowds, he went up on the mountain, and when he sat down, his disciples came to him. And he opened his mouth and taught them."

Moses climbed Sinai to receive the Law. The new and greater Moses climbs the mountain to give it. The King takes the rabbi's seat. He opens His mouth — a phrase that signals weighty teaching.

The Sermon on the Mount is not Christ giving us a ladder to climb to God. It is Christ describing the citizens of the Kingdom He has come to bring.

## The Beatitudes

> "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."

The first beatitude sets the foundation. *Poor in spirit* — those who know they have nothing to bring, no righteousness of their own, no spiritual riches to boast in. The Kingdom belongs to the empty.

> "Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted."

Mourning over sin. Mourning over a broken world. The King promises comfort.

> "Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth."

Meekness is not weakness. It is strength under control — power restrained by humility.

> "Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they shall be satisfied."

The Kingdom belongs to those who long for what only God can give.

> "Blessed are the merciful, for they shall receive mercy."

> "Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God."

> "Blessed are the peacemakers, for they shall be called sons of God."

> "Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."

**The Promised King declares blessing on the very people the world overlooks.** Poor, mourning, meek, hungry, merciful, pure, peacemaking, persecuted. None of these are praised by the world. All of them are praised by the King.

## Persecuted on My account

He extends the eighth beatitude:

> "Blessed are you when others revile you and persecute you and utter all kinds of evil against you falsely on my account. Rejoice and be glad, for your reward is great in heaven, for so they persecuted the prophets who were before you."

To be hated for Christ is to be in the company of the prophets. The reward is great in heaven.

## Salt and light

> "You are the salt of the earth, but if salt has lost its taste, how shall its saltiness be restored?"

Salt preserves and seasons. The King's people are meant to preserve a decaying world from corruption, and to season it with flavor it could not have without them.

> "You are the light of the world. A city set on a hill cannot be hidden."

Light is not for hiding. It is for shining. **The Promised King calls His disciples to live so visibly for God that the world sees their good works and gives glory to the Father in heaven.**

> "Let your light shine before others, so that they may see your good works and give glory to your Father who is in heaven."

## Not to abolish but to fulfill

> "Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them."

The Promised King is the rightful interpreter and fulfiller of every word ever written by Moses and the prophets. He has not come to lower the bar. He has come to raise it — and to meet it Himself.

> "For truly, I say to you, until heaven and earth pass away, not an iota, not a dot, will pass from the Law until all is accomplished."

> "For I tell you, unless your righteousness exceeds that of the scribes and Pharisees, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven."

This is staggering. The scribes and Pharisees were the most outwardly religious people in Israel. The King says: *unless your righteousness exceeds theirs.* A righteousness that comes not from law-keeping but from the King Himself.

## You have heard… But I say

Six times in this chapter, the King uses a refrain: *You have heard that it was said… but I say to you.*

This is not the King overturning Moses. This is the King exposing how the law had been reduced — going beneath the action to the heart.

> "You have heard that it was said, 'You shall not murder'… But I say to you that everyone who is angry with his brother will be liable to judgment."

Murder begins as anger. The King addresses the root, not just the fruit.

> "You have heard that it was said, 'You shall not commit adultery.' But I say to you that everyone who looks at a woman with lustful intent has already committed adultery with her in his heart."

Adultery begins in the eyes and the imagination. The King addresses where sin really lives — inside.

> "If your right eye causes you to sin, tear it out and throw it away."

He is not commanding mutilation. He is commanding radical seriousness. **Nothing in this life is worth keeping if it costs you the Kingdom.**

## On divorce, oaths, retaliation, enemies

He confronts the casual practice of divorce, the careless use of oaths, the cycle of retaliation, the easy logic of loving only friends.

> "But I say to you, Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you, so that you may be sons of your Father who is in heaven. For he makes his sun rise on the evil and on the good."

This is the climax of the chapter. The Father loves indiscriminately. He sends sun and rain on the just and the unjust. **The Promised King calls His people to love with the same wide arms.**

> "If you love those who love you, what reward do you have? Do not even the tax collectors do the same?"

> "You therefore must be perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect."

The standard is not the religious average. The standard is the Father Himself.

## The impossibility — and the answer

If you read Matthew 5 and feel crushed, you have read it rightly.

The standard is impossible. No one meets it. Not the scribes. Not the Pharisees. Not us. Anger is in every heart. Lust is in every eye. Hatred lurks where love should be.

The only One who ever met the standard of Matthew 5 was the One who preached it.

And He preached it not first as something we must do — but as a description of the Kingdom He came to bring, and of the King who alone embodies it. He fulfills the Law in our place. He gives us His own righteousness. And then, by His Spirit, He begins to remake us from the inside out, so that the Beatitudes start to be true of us — not as performance, but as fruit.

The Promised King has spoken. **The constitution of the Kingdom has been read aloud.** And only one Man in history has ever lived inside it perfectly.

---

## Closing prayer

Father, save us from a small reading of this Sermon — a reading that makes Christ a self-improvement teacher. Make us first poor in spirit, knowing we cannot meet His standard. Then clothe us in the righteousness of the One who meets it for us. Make us salt and light. Make us love our enemies and pray for those who persecute us. And let our lives shine in such a way that others give glory to You. In Jesus' name, amen.
