# The Promised King: The Hidden Life of the Kingdom

Matthew 6 continues the Sermon on the Mount.

If Matthew 5 was about the *constitution* of the Kingdom — the kind of righteousness the King requires — Matthew 6 is about the *practice* of the Kingdom. How do you give? How do you pray? How do you fast? Where do you store treasure? What do you do with anxiety?

The King's repeated theme in this chapter is *the Father who sees in secret.* The hidden life is the real life. The reward that matters is from the Father, not from human applause.

The Promised King teaches His people to live before an audience of One.

## Beware of practicing your righteousness before others

> "Beware of practicing your righteousness before other people in order to be seen by them, for then you will have no reward from your Father who is in heaven."

This is the heading of the chapter. Religious activity done to be seen has its reward in the seeing. No further reward awaits.

The King applies this to the three classic practices of devotion: giving, prayer, fasting. In each case, He contrasts the hypocrite who performs publicly and the disciple who acts in secret.

## Giving in secret

> "When you give to the needy, sound no trumpet before you, as the hypocrites do in the synagogues and in the streets, that they may be praised by others."

He says simply:

> "But when you give to the needy, do not let your left hand know what your right hand is doing, so that your giving may be in secret. And your Father who sees in secret will reward you."

**The Promised King wants generosity that is not performed.** Where the world wants the gift to be noticed, the Kingdom wants the gift to be hidden.

## Praying in secret

> "And when you pray, you must not be like the hypocrites. For they love to stand and pray in the synagogues and at the street corners, that they may be seen by others. Truly, I say to you, they have received their reward."

He gives a positive instruction:

> "When you pray, go into your room and shut the door and pray to your Father who is in secret. And your Father who sees in secret will reward you."

Then He warns against another error — not hypocrisy, but empty repetition:

> "And when you pray, do not heap up empty phrases as the Gentiles do, for they think that they will be heard for their many words. Do not be like them, for your Father knows what you need before you ask him."

## The Lord's Prayer

Then He gives the prayer that the church has prayed for two thousand years:

> "Pray then like this:
> Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name.
> Your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven.
> Give us this day our daily bread,
> and forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors.
> And lead us not into temptation, but deliver us from evil."

Six petitions. The first three are about God — His name, His Kingdom, His will. The last three are about us — bread, forgiveness, deliverance.

The order matters. **The Promised King teaches us to begin with the Father, not with our list.**

He adds a sober word about forgiveness:

> "For if you forgive others their trespasses, your heavenly Father will also forgive you, but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father forgive your trespasses."

We who have been forgiven much cannot withhold forgiveness from others. To refuse forgiveness is to reveal that we have not understood our own.

## Fasting in secret

> "When you fast, do not look gloomy like the hypocrites, for they disfigure their faces that their fasting may be seen by others… But when you fast, anoint your head and wash your face, that your fasting may not be seen by others but by your Father who is in secret."

In all three — giving, praying, fasting — the same pattern. The hypocrite seeks human notice. The disciple seeks the Father's reward.

## Treasures in heaven

The King turns to the question of what we store up:

> "Do not lay up for yourselves treasures on earth, where moth and rust destroy and where thieves break in and steal, but lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven, where neither moth nor rust destroys and where thieves do not break in and steal."

He gives the reason:

> "For where your treasure is, there your heart will be also."

**The treasure tells the truth about the heart.** Wherever your money goes, your heart will follow. You can know what you really love by tracing where your treasure has gone.

## The lamp of the body

> "The eye is the lamp of the body. So, if your eye is healthy, your whole body will be full of light, but if your eye is bad, your whole body will be full of darkness."

What you fix your eye on transforms you. Watch carefully what you behold.

## Two masters

> "No one can serve two masters, for either he will hate the one and love the other, or he will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve God and money."

This is one of the sharpest sayings in the Gospel. **There is no negotiated peace between God and money.** One will rule. The other will be despised. The King is honest. There is no third way.

## Do not be anxious

The chapter climbs to one of the most beloved passages in the Bible.

> "Therefore I tell you, do not be anxious about your life, what you will eat or what you will drink, nor about your body, what you will put on. Is not life more than food, and the body more than clothing?"

The King points to nature:

> "Look at the birds of the air: they neither sow nor reap nor gather into barns, and yet your heavenly Father feeds them. Are you not of more value than they?"

> "And why are you anxious about clothing? Consider the lilies of the field, how they grow: they neither toil nor spin, yet I tell you, even Solomon in all his glory was not arrayed like one of these."

If God clothes the grass — here today, gone tomorrow — how much more will He clothe His own people?

> "Therefore do not be anxious, saying, 'What shall we eat?' or 'What shall we drink?' or 'What shall we wear?' For the Gentiles seek after all these things, and your heavenly Father knows that you need them all."

Anxiety is not a Christian virtue. It is the way of those without a Father. **The Promised King teaches His people to live like children of a Father who knows what they need.**

## Seek first the Kingdom

The climax:

> "But seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness, and all these things will be added to you."

Put the Kingdom first. Everything else follows.

> "Therefore do not be anxious about tomorrow, for tomorrow will be anxious for itself. Sufficient for the day is its own trouble."

Today is enough. Tomorrow will come with its own grace. **The Promised King teaches His people to live one day at a time, trusting the Father for the next.**

---

## Closing prayer

Father, save us from religion that performs for human eyes. Teach us to give, pray, and fast in secret, before You alone. Let our prayers begin with Your name, Your Kingdom, Your will — and then trust You for daily bread, forgiveness, and deliverance. Pull our hearts away from earthly treasure to treasure in heaven. Free us from anxiety, and teach us to seek first Your Kingdom — knowing that our Father knows what we need. In Jesus' name, amen.
