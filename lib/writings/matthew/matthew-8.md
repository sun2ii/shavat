# The Promised King: Authority Over Disease, Distance, and Nature

Matthew has just given us three chapters of the King's teaching. Now he gives us three chapters of the King's deeds.

Matthew 8 begins with three healings in rapid succession — a leper, a Gentile's servant, Peter's mother-in-law — and continues with a storm stilled, demons cast out into pigs, and a moment of confrontation with would-be disciples about the cost of following.

The point: the King who spoke with authority in chapters 5–7 acts with authority in chapters 8–9. **His word is not theory. It commands sickness, distance, weather, and demons — and they all obey.**

## A leper made clean

When He comes down from the mountain, great crowds follow Him. Behold, a leper comes to Him and kneels:

> "Lord, if you will, you can make me clean."

A leper. The most isolated person in Israel. Untouchable. Excluded from the camp. The Law required him to live outside, calling out *unclean* if anyone came near.

Jesus stretches out His hand and touches him:

> "I will; be clean."

Immediately his leprosy is cleansed.

**The Promised King does not keep His distance from the untouchable. He touches first.** The hand that should have become unclean by contact instead made the leper clean. The flow of contamination is reversed.

He says: *See that you say nothing to anyone, but go, show yourself to the priest and offer the gift that Moses commanded, for a proof to them.* The King is not seeking publicity. He is seeking obedience to the Law that He Himself wrote — and quiet testimony to those who needed to see it.

## A centurion's faith

When He enters Capernaum, a centurion comes forward, appealing to Him:

> "Lord, my servant is lying paralyzed at home, suffering terribly."

Jesus says: "I will come and heal him."

The centurion answers:

> "Lord, I am not worthy to have you come under my roof, but only say the word, and my servant will be healed. For I too am a man under authority, with soldiers under me. And I say to one, 'Go,' and he goes; and to another, 'Come,' and he comes; and to my servant, 'Do this,' and he does it."

The centurion is a Roman officer. A Gentile. An occupier. He understands one thing very well: authority. He has it. He recognizes it.

He sees in Jesus an Authority higher than his own — high enough that a single word will heal his servant from a distance.

When Jesus hears this, He marvels:

> "Truly, I tell you, with no one in Israel have I found such faith. I tell you, many will come from east and west and recline at table with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob in the kingdom of heaven, while the sons of the kingdom will be thrown into the outer darkness."

**The Promised King praises a Gentile soldier's faith above any faith He has found in Israel.** And He gives a vision: many Gentiles will come from east and west and feast in the Kingdom, while many who assumed they belonged will be cast out.

He says to the centurion: *Go; let it be done for you as you have believed.* The servant is healed at that very moment.

## Peter's mother-in-law

He enters Peter's house and sees his mother-in-law lying sick with a fever. He touches her hand, and the fever leaves her. She rises and serves Him.

That evening they bring to Him many who are oppressed by demons. He casts out the spirits with a word and heals all who are sick.

This fulfills Isaiah:

> "He took our illnesses and bore our diseases."

**The Promised King's healings are not just acts of compassion. They are previews of the cross. He bears our diseases.** Every healing in the Gospels is a small picture of what the great healing will be at Calvary.

## The cost of following

When Jesus sees a crowd around Him, He gives orders to go over to the other side.

A scribe comes up:

> "Teacher, I will follow you wherever you go."

Jesus answers:

> "Foxes have holes, and birds of the air have nests, but the Son of Man has nowhere to lay his head."

The King has nowhere to sleep. The scribe must know what he is signing up for.

Another of His disciples says: *Lord, let me first go and bury my father.*

Jesus answers:

> "Follow me, and leave the dead to bury their own dead."

This sounds harsh. But the King will not be put in second place behind even the most legitimate family obligations. **Following Him cannot be postponed.**

## The storm

He gets into the boat. His disciples follow. Behold, a great storm arises on the sea, so that the boat is being swamped by the waves. But He is asleep.

They go and wake Him:

> "Save us, Lord; we are perishing."

He says:

> "Why are you afraid, O you of little faith?"

Then He rises and rebukes the winds and the sea. There is a great calm.

The men marvel:

> "What sort of man is this, that even winds and sea obey him?"

**The Promised King has authority not just over disease and distance, but over the very forces of nature.** Only the Creator commands the wind.

The Old Testament again and again celebrates the LORD as the One who stills the storm. Now a Man in a Galilean fishing boat speaks one word and the sea obeys Him. The disciples are right to ask: *what sort of Man is this?* He is the King — and more than the King. He is *Immanuel*. God with us.

## The demons and the pigs

When He comes to the other side, to the country of the Gadarenes, two demon-possessed men meet Him, coming out of the tombs. They are so fierce that no one can pass that way.

They cry out:

> "What have you to do with us, O Son of God? Have you come here to torment us before the time?"

Even the demons know who He is. They have right doctrine — and no salvation.

A herd of pigs feeds at some distance. The demons beg Him: *If you cast us out, send us away into the herd of pigs.* He says: *Go.*

They come out and go into the pigs. The whole herd rushes down the steep bank into the sea and drowns.

The herdsmen flee, and going into the city, they tell everything. All the city comes out to meet Jesus.

And when they see Him, they beg Him to leave their region.

**The Promised King has delivered two demon-possessed men — but the city loves its pigs more than its people.** They ask Him to leave. He does.

This is the great tragedy of much of Israel in Matthew's Gospel — and of much of the world today. **The King comes. The King heals. The King brings freedom. And the people, more concerned with their property than with their salvation, ask Him to go.**

## Closing prayer

Father, thank You that the Promised King has authority over leprosy and fever, over distance and demons, over wind and waves. Thank You that He stretched out His hand and touched the untouchable, and praised the faith of an outsider. Save us from being like the Gadarenes, who valued their pigs more than their King. And give us faith like the centurion — to trust that one word from Christ is enough. In Jesus' name, amen.
